
Half a century has passed since Theodore Maiman, with his small ruby rod, realized a breakthrough in laser emission. This remarkable achievement captured attention in many publications, but, in the initial years, the laser was described as “a solution in search of a problem,” and there was, perhaps, some truth in this observation. Years of development have significantly enhanced laser performance and greatly expanded their variety, allowing lasers to assume important roles in scientific research, consumer products, telecommunications, engineering, medicine, materials processing, and numerous other fields. This article aims to review the principal developments and to place them in context, illustrating how laser technology has evolved to satisfy the demands of diverse applications.

The laser is frequently associated with the transistor and the computer as fundamental inventions of the mid-20th century. These three technologies, with profound conceptual origins, flourished rapidly after the conclusion of World War II. They benefited from substantial investments by governments and corporations in physical research, an increase in the number of physicists and engineers, and a heritage of ideas and instruments from the war period. It was an era characterized by notable technological progress and optimism, yet tempered by concerns over Cold War tensions, which could have escalated into nuclear conflict.

Although the potential of these technologies appears evident today, it was not immediately clear. The transistor was initially regarded as a compact substitute for vacuum tubes, and integrated circuits were developed only after more than a decade. Programmable electronic computers were considered useful solely for scientific investigations. As for the laser, at its inception, it was variously celebrated as a science-fiction death ray or a coherent transmitter for atmospheric communications.

In examining the evolution of laser science and technology, this article concentrates on two interconnected series of developments: the creation of laser devices and their applications. Naturally, these two aspects influenced one another. Developers of applications tested existing laser devices and offered feedback to laser engineers regarding features necessary to render applications effective. As applications advanced, their requirements for lasers also evolved. For instance, early optical communication systems stimulated the development of semiconductor diode lasers. Gallium-arsenide diodes sufficed for connections spanning several kilometers between telephone offices, but, when glass fibers were discovered to transmit more effectively at longer wavelengths, InGaAsP diode lasers were developed for the windows at 1310 and 1550 nm. Thus, laser technology progressed in response to economic and practical necessities, in a manner similar to other technologies.

Lasers have experienced a rich and complex history over the half-century since Theodore Maiman inaugurated the laser era on May 16, 1960, at Hughes Research Laboratories. This article cannot fully explore that history with the depth it merits. It is challenging to enumerate all significant developments of the past fifty years or to acknowledge every individual who contributed meaningfully. Instead, the focus is on general trends in the technology, highlighting certain milestones, with the hope that no essential aspects are overlooked. The first conceptual foundation of the laser was Albert Einstein’s 1916 proposal that photons could stimulate the emission of identical photons from excited atoms. In 1928, Rudolf Ladenburg provided indirect evidence of stimulated emission. However, physicists of the time referred to it as “negative absorption” and deemed it of little practical significance, expecting Boltzmann population distributions to prevail, with higher energy states less populated than lower ones.

In 1940, Russian physicist Valentin A. Fabrikant suggested that stimulated emission in a gas discharge could amplify light under appropriate conditions. Yet, he did not propose a resonator and did not pursue the idea further. After World War II, Willis Lamb, Jr., and R. C. Retherford recognized that nuclear magnetic resonance could generate population inversions, and Edward M. Purcell and Robert V. Pound employed this effect to observe stimulated emission of 50-kHz radio waves.

In 1951, Charles H. Townes advanced the concept, proposing that stimulated emission at microwave frequencies could oscillate in a resonant cavity, producing coherent output. In 1954, Townes and his student James Gordon demonstrated the first microwave maser, directing excited ammonia molecules into a resonant cavity where they oscillated at 24 GHz.

A surge of microwave maser development ensued, but some physicists began to consider extending the maser principle to higher frequencies. With millimeter waves, the terahertz band, and the far infrared largely unexplored, this implied a leap to optical frequencies, three to four orders of magnitude higher. Initially, Townes dismissed the notion, but, by the summer of 1957, he reconsidered and began a systematic investigation. He consulted colleagues at Columbia University and, shortly after the Sputnik launch on October 4, met with Gordon Gould, a 37-year-old doctoral student working under Polykarp Kusch.

At this juncture, Townes had articulated a physics challenge—how to construct an optical oscillator to generate coherent light by amplifying stimulated emission. For his dissertation, Gould was employing optical pumping to measure properties of thallium vapor. Townes believed optical pumping might produce the population inversion required for his optical maser, so he inquired about Gould’s thallium lamp. Gould, in response, asked about Townes’s project, and, upon learning of it, revealed he had been contemplating a similar idea. After a second discussion, they pursued the problem independently. Both succeeded.

Gould, with aspirations of becoming an inventor, had prior experience in optics. He secluded himself with references, coined the term “laser,” and outlined a plan for the Fabry-Pérot resonator in a notebook notarized on November 13, 1957. That notebook later formed the basis for a prolonged patent dispute, which ultimately made Gould a multimillionaire.

Townes collaborated with Arthur Schawlow, a former Columbia colleague who had married Townes’s sister and specialized in optical spectroscopy. Together, they authored a comprehensive proposal for an “optical maser,” published in Physical Review in December 1958.

The race to construct a laser commenced, but two critical questions remained: how to excite a population inversion and what to use as an active medium. Schawlow and Townes focused on optical pumping of a vaporized alkali metal, such as potassium, with a lamp emitting on the same element’s lines. Their paper also mentioned optical pumping of an impurity atom in a transparent solid, but they believed this would require a precisely matched light source. Ali Javan at Bell Labs proposed exciting a gas with an electric discharge, using helium to transfer energy to neon atoms that emitted light. Gould included these possibilities in his patent application. Experimental progress, however, remained slow.

Maiman investigated ruby, a material he knew well from designing a compact microwave maser with ruby crystals. Schawlow had dismissed ruby as unsuitable for lasers, citing its three-level system and perceived inefficient red fluorescence. Maiman’s measurements revealed that ruby fluorescence was, in fact, efficient, and he determined that intense white-light lamps could excite chromium atoms in ruby to the laser level, particularly with bright flashlamp pulses.

Maiman’s ruby laser appeared deceptively simple. By placing a small ruby rod within the coil of a photographic flashlamp, enclosed in a reflective cylinder, he concentrated intense pump light into the rod. On May 16, 1960, he tested the design, gradually increasing the flashlamp voltage until the red light pulses intensified sharply, with time and spectral profiles confirming laser action.

Hughes announced the laser at a press conference in New York on July 7, 1960, after Physical Review Letters rejected Maiman’s report. Some researchers expressed skepticism, but Gould’s colleagues at TRG Inc. and Schawlow’s at Bell Labs constructed working ruby lasers within weeks, relying solely on the Hughes press release photo. Maiman published a brief account in Nature, with a more detailed report appearing in 1961.

The ruby laser astonished most researchers but inspired Peter Sorokin and Mirek Stevenson. They used flashlamp-pumped calcium fluoride crystals doped with uranium, identified as a four-level laser system, to create the second laser, followed by a third using samarium-doped CaF2. Unlike ruby, these required cryogenic cooling and emitted in the infrared, finding no practical applications.

A noteworthy historical detail is the red ruby laser, demonstrated independently by Schawlow at Bell and Irwin Wieder at Varian Associates, with papers published together in Physical Review Letters in 1960. Maiman’s laser used “pink” ruby with low chromium concentration. At higher concentrations, red ruby formed a four-level system with emission at 701.0 and 704.1 nm when cooled to liquid nitrogen temperature. However, like the uranium and samarium lasers, it proved impractical.

Ali Javan, William Bennett, and Donald Herriott constructed a high-reflectivity cavity, about a meter long, for the low-gain helium-neon laser, succeeding on December 12, 1960. Operating at 1.15 μm, it was the first continuous-wave and gas laser, closer to the original vision of a coherent optical oscillator, though significantly longer than the 10-cm cavity Schawlow and Townes had envisioned.
