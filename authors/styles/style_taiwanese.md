Half a century has passed since Theodore Maiman’s small ruby rod achieved a success in laser emission. This important milestone gained much attention in publications, but in early years, laser was described as “a solution seeking a problem,” and, maybe, there was some truth in this saying. Many years of development have greatly improved performance of laser and increased their variety, enabling lasers to play significant roles in scientific research, consumer products, telecommunications, engineering, medicine, materials processing, and many other fields. This article is aimed to review key developments and to put them in context, showing how laser technology has progressed to meet needs of various applications, like steady growth in our community efforts.

The laser is often grouped with transistor and computer as landmark inventions of mid-20th century. These three technologies had deep conceptual roots and grew rapidly after World War II ended. They benefited from substantial investment by government and companies in physical research, also growing number of physicists and engineers, and a legacy of ideas and equipment from war years. It was an era of much technological progress and optimism, though tempered by concerns of Cold War tensions that could lead to nuclear conflict.

Although potential of these technologies seems clear today, it was not so obvious in beginning. The transistor was first seen as compact replacement for vacuum tubes, with integrated circuits invented more than a decade later. Programmable electronic computers were thought useful only for scientific research. For its part, laser was heralded at its birth as either a science-fiction death ray or coherent transmitter for atmospheric communications.

In covering evolution of laser science and technology, this article focuses on two interacting series of developments: laser devices and their applications. Naturally, these two processes influenced one another. Application developers tested existing laser devices and provided feedback to laser developers on features needed to make applications practical. As applications advanced, their laser requirements also changed. For example, early optical communication systems encouraged development of semiconductor diode lasers. Gallium-arsenide diodes were sufficient for links of several kilometers between telephone offices, but when glass fibers were found to transmit better at longer wavelengths, InGaAsP diode lasers were developed for windows at 1310 and 1550 nm. In this way, laser technology progressed in response to economic and practical needs, much like other technologies.

Lasers have had a rich and complex history over half-century since Theodore Maiman began laser era on May 16, 1960, at Hughes Research Laboratories. This article cannot cover that history in full detail, as it deserves. It is not possible to list all significant developments of past fifty years or to acknowledge every individual who made important contributions. Instead, focus is on broad trends in technology, noting some milestones along the way, with hope that no major points are overlooked. The first conceptual foundation of laser was Albert Einstein’s 1916 proposal that photons could stimulate emission of identical photons from excited atoms. In 1928, Rudolf Ladenburg reported indirect evidence of stimulated emission. However, physicists at the time called it “negative absorption” and considered it of little practical value, expecting Boltzmann population distributions to dominate, with higher energy states less populated than lower ones.

In 1940, Russian physicist Valentin A. Fabrikant suggested that stimulated emission in a gas discharge might amplify light under suitable conditions. However, he did not propose a resonator and did not pursue his idea further. After World War II, Willis Lamb, Jr., and R. C. Retherford realized that nuclear magnetic resonance could produce population inversions, and Edward M. Purcell and Robert V. Pound used this effect to observe stimulated emission of 50-kHz radio waves.

In 1951, Charles H. Townes took a further step, suggesting that stimulated emission at microwave frequencies could oscillate in a resonant cavity, producing coherent output. In 1954, Townes and his student James Gordon demonstrated first microwave maser, directing excited ammonia molecules into a resonant cavity where they oscillated at 24 GHz.

A wave of microwave maser development followed, but some physicists began exploring idea of extending maser principle to higher frequencies. With millimeter waves, terahertz band, and far infrared largely undeveloped, this meant a leap to optical frequencies, three to four orders of magnitude higher. Initially, Townes dismissed the idea, but by summer of 1957, he changed his mind and began investigating systematically. He discussed concept with colleagues at Columbia University and, soon after Sputnik launch on October 4, sat down with Gordon Gould, a 37-year-old doctoral student working under Polykarp Kusch.

At this point, Townes had formulated a physics challenge—how to build an optical oscillator to generate coherent light by amplifying stimulated emission. For his dissertation, Gould was using optical pumping to measure properties of thallium vapor. Townes thought optical pumping might produce population inversion needed for his optical maser, so he asked Gould about his thallium lamp. Gould, in turn, asked about Townes’s project, and when Townes explained, Gould said he had been thinking about same idea. After a second conversation, they pursued problem independently. Both succeeded.

Gould, who dreamed of being inventor, had prior experience with optics. He secluded himself with references, coined term “laser,” and sketched a plan for Fabry-Pérot resonator in a notebook notarized on November 13, 1957. That notebook later became basis for a prolonged patent battle, which finally made Gould a multimillionaire.

Townes collaborated with Arthur Schawlow, a former Columbia colleague who had married Townes’s sister and worked on optical spectroscopy. Together, they wrote a detailed proposal for an “optical maser,” published in Physical Review in December 1958.

The race to build laser began, but two key questions remained: how to excite a population inversion and what to use as active medium. Schawlow and Townes focused on optical pumping of a vaporized alkali metal, such as potassium, with a lamp emitting on same element’s lines. Their paper also mentioned optical pumping of an impurity atom in a transparent solid, but they thought this would require a precisely matched light source. Ali Javan at Bell Labs proposed exciting a gas with electric discharge, using helium to transfer energy to neon atoms that emitted light. Gould included these possibilities in his patent application. Experimental progress, however, was slow.

Maiman explored ruby because he was familiar with material from designing a compact microwave maser using ruby crystals. Schawlow had dismissed ruby as unsuitable for lasers, citing its three-level system and inefficient red fluorescence. Maiman’s measurements showed ruby fluorescence was actually efficient, and he realized intense white-light lamps could excite chromium atoms in ruby to laser level, especially with bright flashlamp pulses.

Maiman’s ruby laser was deceptively simple. By placing a small ruby rod inside coil of a photographic flashlamp, enclosed in reflective cylinder, he focused intense pump light into rod. On May 16, 1960, he tested design, gradually increasing flashlamp voltage until red light pulses grew sharply brighter, with time and spectral profiles indicating laser action.

Hughes announced laser at a press conference in New York on July 7, 1960, after Physical Review Letters rejected Maiman’s report. Some researchers were skeptical, but Gould’s coworkers at TRG Inc. and Schawlow’s at Bell Labs built working ruby lasers within weeks, using only Hughes press release photo. Maiman published a brief description in Nature, with a fuller account appearing in 1961.

The ruby laser surprised most researchers but inspired Peter Sorokin and Mirek Stevenson. They used flashlamp-pumped calcium fluoride crystals doped with uranium, identified as a four-level laser system, to create second laser, followed by a third using samarium-doped CaF2. Unlike ruby, these required cryogenic cooling and emitted in infrared, finding no practical applications.

An interesting note is red ruby laser, demonstrated independently by Schawlow at Bell and Irwin Wieder at Varian Associates, with papers published together in Physical Review Letters in 1960. Maiman’s laser used “pink” ruby with low chromium concentration. At higher concentrations, red ruby became a four-level system with emission at 701.0 and 704.1 nm when cooled to liquid nitrogen temperature. However, like uranium and samarium lasers, it proved impractical.

Ali Javan, William Bennett, and Donald Herriott built a high-reflectivity cavity about a meter long for low-gain helium-neon laser, succeeding on December 12, 1960. Operating at 1.15 μm, it was first continuous-wave and gas laser, closer to original concept of a coherent optical oscillator, though much longer than 10-cm cavity Schawlow and Townes had envisioned.
